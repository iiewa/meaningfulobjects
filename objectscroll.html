I’m not religious and I’m don’t think I’m spiritual either but Buddhism meant a lot to my grandmother and impacted my mother’s childhood. Those things I found myself thinking about.

CHILDHOOD (AND CURRENT) HOUSE

230400 ”

7370 DAYS

I was raised by my grandmother, my great grandmother who was 92 years old, and my mom is in that house and now we take care of my grandmother so it’s become a full cycle. And that’s the house that my dad lived in before he passed away.

We have a garage that has been accumulating stuff for the past 20-something years. To think that my baby clothes are in in there is so weird. You move on, but the house doesn’t unless you make it. It’s interesting because it means so much but at the same time, it can feel kind of regressive because nothing has changed.

I hope I make enough money soon enough after college to buy my house. Because there’s so much history. Even if I flip it, it can be the same. Even if home becomes a different thing for me, like where I choose to work, where I grew up will always be the same place. And if I I’m going back to the same structure, I won’t go home feeling like a stranger.

2191 DAYS

In high school, I started assigning more meaning to my trips to Taiwan and what it meant to me to be Taiwanese—those politics were not taught to me as a kid.

So this necklace represents how much more seriously I started taking those things. And how it became a symbol of community, power in identity, and political power—not just childhood joy.

I also started realizing how important my grandma was and how important it was to me like connected to her. She took care of me and she raised me and I took a lot of it for granted. And so I started calling her like every day on the way to school and trying to build a more active relationship with her.

I think a lot of times what happens especially in diasporic kids is you grow up and they pass away, so you feel removed from them. Your frames of reference are totally different, and also language barriers are a big part of it.
